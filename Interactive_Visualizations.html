The Visual Guide to Inframeasures

Gaining geometric intution in finite dimensions

01Finite Signed Measures & Linear Functionals

$\mathcal{M}^\pm(X)$ — all finite signed measures over $X$.

Borel $\sigma$-Algebra, for us: power set.

$$m: \Sigma(X) \to \mathbb{R}$$

$\sigma$-additivity: $\;m\!\left(\bigcup_i A_i\right) = \sum_i m(A_i),\quad A_i \cap A_j = \emptyset$

$m(\emptyset) = 0, \quad |m(A)| < \infty \;\;\forall A \in \Sigma(X)$

$\longrightarrow$ Vector space.

For us, $\sigma$-additivity implies that $m$ can be uniquely specified by the value it takes on the individual $x \in X$.

$$\mathcal{M}^\pm(X) \cong \mathbb{R}^4, \quad \mathcal{M}^\pm(X) \oplus \mathbb{R} \cong \mathbb{R}^5$$

$m \in \mathcal{M}^\pm(X)$ can be interpreted as a function $m: C(X,[0,1]) \to \mathbb{R},\;\; m(f) = \int\! f\, dm$.

For us:

$$(m,b)(f) \;=\; \sum_i m_{(a_i, r_i)}\, f_{a_i, r_i} \;+\; b$$

$\mathcal{M}^\pm(X)$ is equipped with weak topology: $m_n \to m \iff m_n(f) \to m(f)$ for all $f \in C_b(X)$ (bounded cont. func.).

02The SA-Measure Cone

sa-measure: $(m^+ + m^-,\; b) \in \mathcal{M}^\pm(X) \oplus \mathbb{R}\;$ such that $\;m^-(1) + b \geq 0$.

From Jordan decomposition: $m = m^+ + m^-$, where $m^+ \geq 0,\; m^- \leq 0$.

$$m(f) + b = \begin{pmatrix}m \\ b\end{pmatrix}\!\cdot\!\begin{pmatrix}1 \\ 1\end{pmatrix} \geq 0$$

Why sa-measures like this: $m^-(1) + b \geq 0$?  (And not $m(1) + b \geq 0$?)

Because we want $m(f) + b \geq 0\;\;\forall f \in C(X, [0,1])$.

"$\Rightarrow$": $\;m(f) + b \geq 0 \;\Rightarrow\; m(1 \cdot \mathbb{1}_N) + b \geq b$, $N$ suitable from decomp. $\;\Rightarrow\; m^-(1) + b \geq 0\;\checkmark$

"$\Leftarrow$": $\;0 \leq m^-(1) + b \leq m^-(1) + m^+(1) + b \leq m^-(f) + m^+(f) + b = m(f) + b$

$\longrightarrow m(f) + b \geq 0.$

So sa-measures are precisely those with $(m,b)(f) \geq 0$ for all $f \in C(X, [0,1])$.

$\mathcal{M}^{SA}(X)$ — also a convex cone. The "all-positive section of $\mathcal{M}^\pm(X) \oplus \mathbb{R}$".

The lower boundary is given by $b = 0$, the other boundaries are those with $\exists f$ such that $m(f) = 0$.

$b=0$ $m_1+b=0$ $m_2+b=0$ $m_1+m_2+b=0$

03Partial Order

$\longrightarrow$ Partial order:

$$(m_1, b_1) \geq (m_2, b_2) \;\iff\; m_1(f) + b_1 \geq m_2(f) + b_2 \;\;\forall f \in C(X,[0,1])$$ $$\iff (m_1 - m_2,\; b_1 - b_2)(f) \geq 0$$ $$\iff (m_1 - m_2,\; b_1 - b_2) \in \mathcal{M}^{SA}(X)$$

$\longrightarrow$ One sa-measure "above" another if their difference is another sa-measure.

For any point $(m,b)$ you get all points it above by gluing $\mathcal{M}^{SA}$ on top of it: $\;\{(m,b) + m_{SA} \mid m_{SA} \in \mathcal{M}^{SA}\}$.

You get the points below it by gluing $-\mathcal{M}^{SA}$ on it: $\;\{(m,b) - m_{SA} \mid m_{SA} \in \mathcal{M}^{SA}\}$, which is not a subset of $\mathcal{M}^{SA}$ anymore.

04Minimal & Extremal Points

For some set $B \subseteq \mathcal{M}^{SA}$, we define the expectation:

$$\mathbb{E}_B(f) = \inf_{(m,b) \in B}\; (m(f) + b).$$

So to calculate these, the points "furthest down" in $B$ are most important — the minimal points $B^{\min}$.

$$B^{\min} = B \;\cap\; \bigcap_{(m,b)\in B}\big((m,b) - \mathcal{M}^{SA}\big) \qquad\text{if } B \text{ is closed.}$$

Upper completion: $B^{uc} = B + \mathcal{M}^{SA}$, gluing $\mathcal{M}^{SA}$ on top of all points in $B$.

Minimal points $B^{\min}$ are NOT obtained by scaling $\lambda(m,b)$, lowering $\lambda$ until you reach boundary of $B$, since $B$ does not have to be a convex cone.

Note on minimal points: they form the "cutting surface" where the cone has been cut.

Boundary of $\mathcal{M}^{SA}$ given by $b = 0$ or $m_i = 0$ for some $i$.

Extremal points are those that don't lie on a line between two other points. Let's call the set of these $B^{\text{ext}}$.

1. Extremal points are minimal. ($\Leftarrow$ non-minimal points are non-extremal.)

$\Longrightarrow$ It is enough to keep track of extremal points!

05Geometric Interpretation of $\mathbb{E}_B(f)$

$$(m,b)(f) = m \cdot f + b = \sum_i \mu_i f_i + b = \begin{pmatrix}1 \\ f\end{pmatrix}\!\cdot\!\begin{pmatrix}b \\ \mu\end{pmatrix}$$

So what is the infimum of this? The function $f$ defines a vector $\begin{pmatrix}1 \\ f\end{pmatrix}$. So the infimum is reached by moving as far as possible along $-\begin{pmatrix}1 \\ f\end{pmatrix}$.

Weak minimal boundedness: $f \mapsto \mathbb{E}_B(f)$ is uniformly continuous.

Boundary interpretation:

The boundary of $\mathcal{M}^a(X)$ are those where $b = 0$ or where some $m_i = 0$ $\;\to\;$ some histories get zero measure.

The plane $\begin{pmatrix}b \\ \lambda, 1\end{pmatrix}$ represents the measures that give the same measure/weight to all possible "microstates" / microhistories $\;\hat{=}\;$ maximum uncertainty / indifference between the micro histories.

Moving to the boundaries of $\mathcal{M}^{SA}(X)$ (except the $b=0$ boundary) represents more and more certainty, measure is being concentrated more and more.

06Normalization

Condition: Normalization.

$$\mathbb{E}_B(0) = 0, \qquad \mathbb{E}_B(1) = 1$$

$\mathbb{E}_B(0) = 0 \;\iff\; \inf_B (m(0) + b) = 0 \;=\; \inf_B \begin{pmatrix}1 \\ 0\end{pmatrix}\!\cdot\!\begin{pmatrix}b \\ m\end{pmatrix} = 0$

$\longrightarrow$ $B$ touches the $b = 0$ plane.

$\mathbb{E}_B(1) = 1 \;\iff\; \inf_B \begin{pmatrix}1 \\ 1\end{pmatrix}\!\cdot\!\begin{pmatrix}b \\ m\end{pmatrix} = 1$

$\begin{pmatrix}1 \\ 1\end{pmatrix}\!\cdot\!\begin{pmatrix}1 \\ m\end{pmatrix} = 1$ defines a plane $\perp \begin{pmatrix}1 \\ 1\end{pmatrix}$.

$\longrightarrow$ $B$ touches this plane.

Normalization: Move $B$ down by $\mathbb{E}_B(0)$ (distance from $b=0$ plane), obtaining $B' = B - (0, \mathbb{E}_B(0))$, then rescale by $\frac{1}{\mathbb{E}_{B'}(1)}$ to fulfill the second condition.

07Mixtures of Infradistributions

We need to take a step back and understand mixtures of infradistributions.

Mixing two shapes:

For every point in $A_1$, there is a scaled copy of $A_2$. The union of all of these is the mixture. $A_1$ is like a paintbrush shape that is dragged and scaled according to $A_2$.

$$\alpha A_1 + (1-\alpha) A_2 = \{\alpha a_1 + (1-\alpha) a_2 \mid a_1 \in A_1,\; a_2 \in A_2\}$$

Point furthest out along some direction will be the weighted average of the vectors in $A_1$ and $A_2$ along the same direction away from the center. This is true along all directions.

$\longrightarrow$ Mixture surface is "weighted interpolation" of surfaces of the mixed sets.

$\longrightarrow$ Mixture corresponds to infradistribution interpolating pointwise / geometrically between mixed infradistributions.

Roughly:

Knightian uncertainty $\;\cong\;$ set union within infradistribution, or within the minimal points.

Prob. distr. between infradist.'s $\;\cong\;$ how much the infradist. looks like another one geometrically.

08The Raw Update

Gluing $f$ to $g$ via $L$:

We often imagine that $L \approx 1$ in some region and $L \approx 0$ in the complement, then $f \star^L g$ is obtained by taking $f$ in the subspace where $L \approx 1$ and $g$ where $L \approx 0$ and adding them up.

$H$ is updated by mapping each $m \in H$ to:

$$(m, b) \;\mapsto\; \frac{1}{P_H^s(L)}\left(m \cdot L,\;\; b + m(0 \star^L g)\right)$$

What does $m \cdot L$ do geometrically? $\;0 \leq L \leq 1$, so $m$ is moved closer to the $m$-boundaries of $\mathcal{M}^{SA}$, the stronger for $m_i$, the stronger "microhistory" $i$ is disconfirmed by $L$.

Or: $m \cdot L$ corresponds to element-wise multiplication of $m$ with $L$, which is like multiplication with the matrix $M_L = \text{diag}(L_i, \ldots, L_n)$. Unit sphere gets mapped to ellipsoid with semi-axes $L_i$.

As a matrix:

$$\begin{pmatrix}b'\\m'\end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix}1 & (1{-}L)g \\ 0 & \text{diag}(L)\end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix}b\\m\end{pmatrix}$$

$\longrightarrow$ raw update maps $\mathcal{M}^{SA}$ to (a subset of) $\mathcal{M}^{SA}$.

$\longrightarrow$ Is linear.

$\Longrightarrow$ It is enough to keep track of extremal points!
